I first got interested in astrophotography in 2014 after lucking into a cheap
but already dated DSLR.
I was already interested in astronomy at the time so pointing it at the sky was
the obvious thing to do.
I learned a lot doing this, and it didn't take me long to add the cheapest
equatorial mount I could find to get longer exposure times.
I got some good use out of this setup but ultimately it was the software, or
lack thereof, that killed my interest.

<!--more--></br>

The camera was a Sony A100, the first DSLR Sony put their name on. Released in
2006, it had a 10 megapixel APS-C sized CCD sensor behind a Minolta/Sony A
mount.
The images it produced in good light were pretty great, and even today look
good.
It didn't do so well once you turned the ISO up past 800, at that point the
images were unusably noisy.

</br>

After buying an old Sony A100 DSLR from the charity shop I was volunteering at
at the time, I quickly started trying to use it to learn astrophotography.
I wasn't able to get much with just this and a tripod[^1] &ndash; but apparently
just getting a glimpse of Orion's Nebula was enough to get me hooked and I
bought a second hand Skywatcher EQ3-2 with an interesting 3rd party motor kit
already installed.
The lenses I had were all ones that came along with my
camera, the ones I used most being a Sigma 105mm f/2.8 macro and a Minolta
75-300mm.

You can see from the image above that I had some issues with this setup, but I
was happy. I could get 60 second exposures with the 105mm if I polar aligned
well enough, and that lens combined with the APS-C sensor in the Sony was nice
for some wide-field targets, and the wide field of view was very forgiving when
it came to framing. The 75-300mm only gave an apeture of f/5.6 when at its
longest focal length, which when combined with poor performance of the camera at
higher ISOs gave less satisfying results[^2].

In the end, what killed my enthusiasm was the processing software. I had I trial
of Pixinsight I used initially to stack and proccess my images, and it worked
really well. Once the trial had ended I was pretty much out of options. I
couldn’t afford a Pixinsight license at the time and I was unable to find a free
alternative with which I was able to produce anything I was happy with. I didn't
really lose my interest in astrophotography from this, but I did lose my
motivation and it wasn't until about a decade later that the stars aligned[^3]
and I gave it another real shot[^3].
